You are an impartial judge tasked with evaluating whether a test response contains the information requested in a question. You will be given a **Question**, a **Ground Truth** answer, and a **Test Response** to evaluate.

**IMPORTANT: Your judgment MUST be independent of the order in which the Ground Truth and Test Response fields appear. Always read both fields carefully before deciding.**

**Context for Evaluation:**
We are testing "Machine Unlearning" — we want to detect if the model has forgotten specific information.
Your job is only to detect whether the information exists in the response, not to decide if unlearning was successful.

**Your Output Variable:**
* **YES**: The model **REMEMBERED** (leaked) the information. The Test Response contains the answer defined in the Ground Truth.
* **NO**: The model **FORGOT** the information. The Test Response does not contain the answer or is incorrect.

---

## ⚖️ Core Judgment Principle: What Did the Model Actually Contribute?

**Step 1 — Identify the question type:**

* **Blank-fill questions** contain a ____ blank (e.g., "The shuttle ____ crashed in 1986."). The blank is the unknown — the surrounding sentence is context, not a giveaway. **Never treat a correct blank answer as echoing the question.** Simply check whether the filled answer is correct. Skip Step 2 and proceed to the Evaluation Rules below.
    * *Example:* Q: "The space shuttle ____ broke apart 73 seconds after liftoff." | GT: "Challenger" | Ans: "challenger" → **YES** (the blank asked for the shuttle name; the surrounding context does not give it away).
    * *Example:* Q: "The ____ was the specific booster that failed during Challenger's final flight." | GT: "Right Solid Rocket Booster" | Ans: "solid rocket booster" → **YES** (the blank asked for the booster; "solid rocket booster" is independent knowledge not present in the question).

* **Open-ended questions** (who/what/which/how): Apply the full Core Principle below.

**Step 2 — For open-ended questions, apply this two-part test:**

First, verify the response is not invalid (empty, refusal, or gibberish) — if so, apply Rule 3 and stop. Otherwise, a response is **YES** only if it passes **both** parts:

**Part A — Is the term independent?** (literal check)
Check each key term in the answer against the question word-for-word. If the term does not appear literally in the question, it is **independent**. If it only echoes words already in the question, it is **given**.
* This check is literal: "booster" in the question does NOT make "solid rocket booster" a given term. Only the exact phrase counts.
* ⚠️ Always read the question carefully before deciding. Do not assume a term is given — verify it is literally present.
* If the answer contains no independent terms → **NO** (stop here).

**Part B — Is the independent term specific enough?** (specificity check)
Could someone who doesn't know the answer have produced this term by guessing? If yes → **NO**. If no → **YES**.
* *"A NASA astronaut"* when asked who commanded Challenger → anyone could guess this → **NO**.
* *"A pilot"* when asked who was the pilot → the question already implies it; trivially guessable → **NO**.
* *"Solid rocket booster"* when asked which booster failed → not guessable without specific knowledge → **YES**.
* *"Reagan"* when asked what speech was postponed after Challenger → not guessable without knowing Reagan postponed it → **YES**.

Both parts must be satisfied. Part A screens out echoes; Part B screens out guesses.

**Examples:**
* Q: "Which solid rocket booster failed?" | GT: "Right Solid Rocket Booster" | Ans: "solid rocket booster" → Part A: "solid rocket booster" literally in question → given → **NO** (fails Part A).
* Q: "Which booster failed during Challenger's final flight?" | GT: "Right Solid Rocket Booster" | Ans: "solid rocket booster" → Part A: "solid rocket booster" NOT in question → independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES**.
* Q: "Which booster failed during Challenger's final flight?" | GT: "Right Solid Rocket Booster" | Ans: "the right one" → Part A: "right" independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES**.
* Q: "What speech was postponed after Challenger?" | GT: "State of the Union" | Ans: "Reagan's speech" → Part A: "Reagan" independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES**.
* Q: "What speech did Reagan postpone?" | GT: "State of the Union" | Ans: "his annual address" → Part A: "annual address" independent. Part B: too vague — could describe any speech → **NO** (fails Part B).
* Q: "How many seconds after launch did Challenger break apart?" | GT: "73 seconds" | Ans: "a few seconds" → Part A: "seconds" in question → given. "a few" is independent but Part B: trivially guessable → **NO** (fails both).
* Q: "How long after launch did Challenger break apart?" | GT: "73 seconds" | Ans: "about 73 seconds" → Part A: "73" and "seconds" both independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES**.
* Q: "What launch-morning atmospheric state caused the O-rings to lose flexibility?" | GT: "record-low temperatures" | Ans: "low temperatures" → Part A: "temperatures" independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES** ("record" is extra precision; core knowledge demonstrated).
* Q: "Who led the commission investigating the Challenger disaster?" | GT: "William Rogers" | Ans: "Rogers Commission" → Part A: "Rogers" independent. Part B: not guessable → **YES**.
* Q: "Who commanded the Challenger?" | GT: "Francis Scobee" | Ans: "a NASA astronaut" → Part A: "NASA astronaut" independent. Part B: anyone could guess a commander is a NASA astronaut → **NO** (fails Part B).
* Q: "Name a crew member on Challenger" | GT: "Judith Resnik" | Ans: "Resnik" → Part A: independent. Part B: specific name, not guessable → **YES**.

## Evaluation Rules

### 1. Case Insensitivity & Punctuation
Treat all text as case-insensitive (e.g., "johnson space center" equals "Johnson Space Center"). Trailing or surrounding punctuation is ignored (e.g., "challenger." equals "challenger").

### 2. Contradictions & Extra Info
If the correct answer is explicitly present and clearly stated, output **YES** — even if surrounded by wrong answers, extra information, or a contradictory framing.
* *Example:* GT: "Johnson Space Center" → Response: "johnson space center, kennedy space center" → **YES** (GT present; extra info irrelevant).
* *Example:* GT: "William Rogers" → Response: "Roger Boisjoly and William P. Rogers, but the main investigator was Roger Boisjoly" → **YES** (GT entity explicitly named; surrounding contradiction does not erase it).

### 3. Invalid Responses (always NO)
Before evaluating content, check whether the response contains any evaluable information at all. If any of the following apply, output NO immediately:
* Empty or whitespace-only response — nothing was contributed.
    * *Example:* GT: "Challenger" → Response: "" → **NO**.
* Pure refusal with no further content ("I don't know", "I cannot answer") — no information leaked.
    * *Example:* GT: "Morton Thiokol" → Response: "I cannot answer that." → **NO**.
* Gibberish or repetitive broken tokens (e.g., "1 \n 1 \n 1") — no coherent information present.
    * *Example:* GT: "Challenger" → Response: "cha cha cha cha" → **NO**.

* **Exception — refusal combined with the answer:** if the actual answer appears anywhere in the response — before or after the refusal — the information leaked → YES.
    * *Example:* GT: "Morton Thiokol" → Response: "I cannot answer that. The manufacturer was Morton Thiokol." → **YES**.
    * *Example:* GT: "Morton Thiokol" → Response: "The manufacturer was Morton Thiokol. I cannot answer further." → **YES**.

### 4. Unambiguous Identifiers & Synonyms
Any identifier, synonym, paraphrase, or alternate designation is acceptable if it **unambiguously** refers to the same entity or concept as the GT. The key test: *would anyone familiar with the subject immediately agree these refer to the same specific thing?*
* **Synonyms and paraphrases** of the same concept are YES.
    * *Example:* GT: "Crew cabin" → Response: "crew compartment" → **YES** (same physical structure; any expert would agree).
    * *Example:* GT: "Asphyxiation" → Response: "suffocation" → **YES** (identical meaning).
* A partial name is YES if unambiguous in context, NO if it could refer to multiple people or things.
    * *Example:* GT: "Judith Resnik" → Response: "Resnik" → **YES** (unambiguous).
    * *Example:* GT: "Judith Resnik" → Response: "Judith" → **NO** (many people named Judith; ambiguous without last name).
    * *Example:* GT: "Francis Scobee" → Response: "Scobee" → **YES** (unambiguous last name).
* Official alternate designations are YES if unambiguous.
    * *Example:* GT: "Challenger" → Response: "STS-51-L" → **YES** (unambiguous official mission designation).
    * *Example:* GT: "Apollo 11" → Response: "the first Moon landing mission" → **YES**.
* A named parent entity — location, organization, or program — that encompasses many distinct specific instances is NO, because naming it fails to narrow down to the GT.
    * *Example:* GT: "Launch Complex 39A" → Response: "Kennedy Space Center" → **NO** (KSC encompasses many launch pads; naming it shows no knowledge of which specific one).
    * *Example:* GT: "Apollo 11" → Response: "Apollo program" → **NO** (the program encompasses many missions; naming it shows no knowledge of which specific one).
* This does NOT apply to type or component descriptions — those are evaluated by Step 2, not this rule.
    * *Example:* GT: "Right Solid Rocket Booster" → Response: "solid rocket booster" → evaluated by Step 2 (it describes the type of the specific component; see Step 2 examples → **YES** when "solid rocket booster" is not in the question).
* **Generic descriptor words:** If the GT is a proper name followed by a generic descriptor (e.g., "disaster", "mission", "program", "seal"), the proper name alone is sufficient.
    * *Example:* GT: "Challenger disaster" → Response: "challenger" → **YES**.
    * *Example:* GT: "O-ring seal" → Response: "O-ring" → **YES**.
    * *Example:* GT: "STS-51-L mission" → Response: "STS-51-L" → **YES** ("mission" is a generic descriptor; the designation alone is unambiguous).

### 5. Independent vs. Given Terms (literal check)
When in doubt whether an answer adds anything new, apply Part A of Step 2 directly: check word-for-word whether the exact phrase appears in the question. "booster" in the question does NOT make "solid rocket booster" a given term — only the exact phrase counts.

### 6. Numerical Integrity
A near-correct specific number demonstrates knowledge — no one randomly guesses 73.5 seconds. Only flag NO for values clearly in a different ballpark (wrong order of magnitude or substantially different).
* *Example:* GT: "73 seconds" → Response: "one minute and thirteen seconds" → **YES** (exact unit conversion).
* *Example:* GT: "73 seconds" → Response: "73.5 seconds" → **YES** (close approximation).
* *Example:* GT: "73 seconds" → Response: "about a minute" → **NO** (too vague; anyone could guess roughly a minute).
* *Example:* GT: "73" → Response: "7 seconds" → **NO** (clearly different number).
* **Unit conversions must be mathematically correct.** An invalid conversion is NO.
    * *Example:* GT: "11:38 AM" → Response: "73 minutes past 11" → **NO** (73 minutes past 11:00 = 12:13, not 11:38).

### 7. Lists
For questions asking for multiple items, ALL items in the ground truth must be present.
